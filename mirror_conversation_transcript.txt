--- CHATGPT (Response 2) ---
You’re right on both points—and this is where things get a bit counterintuitive in a fun way. Yes, photons are massless . They have zero rest mass . But that doesn’t mean they have no physical effect. Here’s the key idea: Even without mass, photons still carry energy and momentum . Their energy is given by: E = h f E = h f E = h f (Planck’s constant × frequency) Their momentum is: p = E c p = \frac{E}{c} p = c E ​ So when light hits a mirror, the photons transfer momentum , not mass. What happens at the mirror When a photon strikes the mirror: It gets absorbed briefly by the electrons in the material Then it’s re-emitted (which is what we perceive as reflection) During this interaction, momentum is transferred to the mirror Because the photon reverses direction when it reflects, the change in momentum is actually doubled. That means the mirror gets a tiny “push.” Does that push matter? In everyday life, it’s way too small to notice. But it’s real. In fact: This effect is called radiation pressure It’s used in concepts like solar sails for spacecraft, where sunlight can literally push a spacecraft through space So the takeaway Photons: no mass ✅ Still carry energy and momentum ✅ Can exert force on objects like mirrors ✅ It’s a good example of how in physics, mass isn’t the only thing that can cause motion or force . If you want, we can go a step deeper into why massless particles can still have momentum—that’s where relativity comes in.

--- USER (Message 3) ---
Interesting. So does that mean a perfect mirror would require perfect arranged atoms? Has anyone done that?

--- CHATGPT (Response 3) ---
Short answer: a “perfect mirror” would require an almost perfectly ordered surface at the atomic scale—and we can get extremely close, but not truly perfect. Here’s how it breaks down. What “perfect” would mean For a mirror to be perfectly reflective, you’d need: A surface that’s flat down to much smaller than the wavelength of light (so, nanometer-scale smoothness for visible light) Uniform atomic arrangement so incoming light waves don’t scatter randomly No absorption (real materials always absorb a tiny bit of energy) No defects, impurities, or thermal vibrations That last one is the real killer—atoms are always moving a little due to temperature. Do we have anything close? Yes—modern engineering gets surprisingly near: Dielectric mirrors (Bragg mirrors) These use many ultra-thin layers of different materials. They can reflect &gt;99.999% of certain wavelengths. They’re not “perfect,” but they’re incredibly efficient. Super-polished metal mirrors Used in telescopes and lasers, with surface roughness down to a few atoms. Cold atom / quantum systems Scientists can arrange atoms in very controlled ways (optical lattices, etc.), but not in a way that creates a perfect macroscopic mirror. Gravitational wave detectors (like LIGO) Their mirrors are among the most perfect objects ever made—polished and coated to extreme precision to minimize any loss or scattering. Why true perfection is basically impossible Thermal motion : atoms vibrate unless you’re at absolute zero (which you can’t fully reach) Quantum effects : there’s always some uncertainty and fluctuation Material limits : every substance absorbs some light A neat twist Interestingly, metals like silver or aluminum don’t reflect because atoms are perfectly arranged—they reflect because their electrons respond collectively to incoming light and re-emit it. So reflection is less about perfect atomic “tiling” and more about how electrons behave. So: We’ve made mirrors that are functionally near-perfect for specific uses, but a truly perfect mirror—reflecting 100% of all light with zero loss—is more of an ideal than something physics lets us fully realize. If you’re curious, there’s an even weirder idea: “perfect mirrors” lead to strange quantum effects like the dynamical Casimir effect —where moving mirrors can actually create photons out of the vacuum.
